A fundamental and unresolved question in neuroscience is how the billions of interconnected neurons in the brain coordinate their activity to support learning, memory, and decision making. Neuromodulators such as dopamine and neural oscillatory activity play key roles in organizing the activities of neurons and in supporting neuroplasticity. Disrupted oscillatory activity during sleep and waking behavior and dopamine release also contribute to disease states such as age-associated cognitive decline, substance abuse, depression, and Parkinson’s disease (PD). My research seeks to understand now neural oscillations, ensemble activity, and dopamine release are coordinated during behavior and sleep to support learning and memory. Towards this end, my laboratory measures single-unit and local-field activity and dopamine release in behaving, sleeping, and anesthetized rats. Our group has also developed novel instrumentation that allows, for the first time, simultaneous measurement of the activities of large groups of neurons and fast, “phasic” changes in dopamine release (Parent et al., 2017). We have also recently integrated Neuropixels probes into our data-acquisition pipeline.

 

Through our investigations of neural synchrony, sleep, and aging, we have found that normal aging is associated with a significant decrease in the frequency of “ripple” oscillations in the hippocampus during slow-wave sleep that are associated with memory formation (Wiegand et al., 2016; Cowen et al., 2018). We have also performed a detailed analysis of how the tuning of single-unit and local-field activity in the hippocampus to locomotor information changes with age (Crown et al., 2022). Our research surrounding Parkinson’s disease has also identified aberrant patterns of sleep-spindle activity in a genetic form of Parkinson’s disease (Crown et al., 2022) and how neural synchrony changes with the development of levodopa-induced dyskinesia and low-dose ketamine administration (Ye et al., 2021).

 

I am particularly excited about my collaboration with Drs. Porreca and Navratilova to investigate how opioids and pain impact sleep physiology. Drs. Porreca and Navratilova have collaborated extensively, and we have co-authored two publications in the journal Pain. To this project, I hope to bring nearly two decades of experience in computational analysis of neural data, oscillatory activity during resting, sleeping, and awake states, and statistics (I regularly teach a graduate-level statistics classes in R) as well as decades of experience in electrophysiology. I have also analyzed sleep/rest EEG in human and animal subjects.

C.      Contributions to Science

 

1.    Aging and Parkinson’s disease are associated with altered single-unit coordination and local-field activity during sleep

The hippocampus is critical for the formation of memories for episodes involving places, events, and time. Episodic memory declines with age, suggesting that age impacts neural processes involved in memory consolidation. Sharp-wave ripples are high-frequency (~150 Hz) oscillations that occur primarily during slow-wave NREM sleep and less frequently during waking immobility. Ripples are generated in the hippocampus, and these oscillations are implicated in the stabilization of long-term memories during sleep. Our analysis of ripples and of correlated single-unit activity in rats during rest identified key age-associated changes that correspond to memory impairment (Wiegand et al.). Specifically, aging was accompanied by a decline in the ripple oscillation frequency, a reduced rate of ripple occurrence, and reduced precision and reliability of neural activity during ripples. Together, these changes may contribute to age-associated memory decline. Interestingly, many of these changes were not found during active waking behavior (Cowen et al. 2022). Furthermore, our examination of local-field activity during locomotor behavior (Crown et al. 2022) revealed that behavior-associated oscillations in the theta and gamma range was less tuned to sensorimotor input in aged animals, suggesting that the aged brain is limited in its capacity to process rapidly changing incoming information.  

As with hippocampal ripples, thalamocortical sleep spindles are also oscillatory events that occur during NREM sleep and thought to be involved in the consolidation of memory. Given evidence for the involvement of spindles in motor memory, we explored whether features of spindles are altered in a genetic form of Parkinson’s disease (Crown et al., 2020). While we found evidence for sleep disruption in Parkinson’s disease, counter to our expectations, we observed that spindle rate and spindle duration were increased in this genetic form of the disease.

2.    Ketamine induces brain-region specific patterns of synchrony and desynchrony

In the last decade, the potential therapeutic applications of ketamine have expanded considerably. For example, hour to days-long exposure to sub-anesthetic ketamine can provide weeks-long management of depression, post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), chronic pain, and L-DOPA-induced dyskinesias (LID). The neural mechanisms by which the therapeutic effects of ketamine are achieved are unknown. Furthermore, little is known about how ketamine alters neural coordination throughout the brain. Understanding the impact of ketamine on neural coordination is important and neural plasticity and communication between brain regions depends on the precise timing of action potentials within and between brain structures. Consequently, we hypothesized that hours-long exposure to ketamine entrains synchronized activity within corticostriatal and hippocampal circuits. To investigate this question, we measure oscillatory in the motor cortex, ventral striatum, dorsal striatum, and hippocampus of behaving rats and found that ketamine induces unique patterns of neural coordination in each brain region. Critically, we found that extended exposure to ketamine hyper-synchronizes corticostriatal circuits at delta (~4 Hz), gamma (~50 Hz), and high-frequency (~140 Hz) bands while activity in the hippocampus became desynchronized or “noisy”. These data suggest that ketamine facilitates communication and plasticity in corticostriatal circuits and disrupts these processes in the hippocampus. Indeed, desynchronous activity in the hippocampus could reduce the strength of associative networks encoding memories for negative events. Disrupting such networks could have positive therapeutic effects in depression and PTSD. An important next step is to determine if ketamine alters coordination in these brain regions though single-unit ensemble recordings.

3.    Anterior-cingulate neurons are involved in post-decision action maintenance and value prediction.

The anterior cingulate cortex, a subregion of the medial prefrontal cortex, plays a critical role in the evaluation of the cost of physical effort. Evidence from rodents and primates suggests that neurons in the anterior cingulate cortex integrate information about expected effort to guide cost-benefit decision making. To identify the physiological correlates of this evaluative process, I used arrays of single-unit electrodes to record ensemble activity in the anterior cingulate cortex as animals made effort- and reward-guided evaluations. Unexpectedly, results indicated that neurons responding to the anticipated effort responded at least 100 milliseconds after animals made their decision, suggesting that these neurons do not contribute to deliberation, but, instead, may be involved in sustaining goal-directed behaviors after decisions are made. Our observations led me to the proposal that the anterior cingulate cortex facilitates “perseverance” by regulating glutamatergic and dopaminergic transmission in the motor cortex and dorsal striatum. This proposal may have significant implications for the study of chronic pain, a condition associated with reduced frontal function. Consequently, my laboratory is now investigating the connection between chronic pain and effort-guided decision making through our collaboration with Dr. Frank Porreca.

4.    Expanding the traditional view of the hippocampal representation of space.

The discovery of the hippocampal place cell (O’Keefe and Dostrovsky, 1971) provided convincing physiological evidence that the hippocampus creates a cognitive map of the environment. Since their original discovery, it has been determined that “place cells” respond to many other behaviorally relevant features such as goals, contexts, trajectories, and the passage of time. My research helped challenge the traditional view of that hippocampal principal cells solely conveyed spatial information. Together with Drew Maurer and Bruce McNaughton, we determined that inhibitory also interneurons convey precise information about space if one considers the phase of the theta rhythm at which interneurons fires3. We used this phase-based definition of the place field to improve existing measures of place-field features2.

 

The second way my research extended the traditional understanding of hippocampal function was through Dr. Douglas Nitz and my investigation of “repeating place fields” - a recently discovered phenomenon whereby multiple place fields appear when animals visit locations with similar behavioral or visual features. I observed that these repeating fields shift forward in space as animals run on spiral-shaped tracks. Further experiments revealed that this shift was most likely due to a buildup of inertial navigation error, suggesting that animals were using an inertial/vestibular strategy as opposed to a visual cue-based navigation strategy – even in brightly-lit rooms. This is an interesting contribution as one assumption in the field was that inertial navigational strategies are only employed when visual cues are unavailable, and our results suggests that the locations of goals can determine which navigation strategy is employed in the hippocampus.

5.    Advancing measurement technologies for the neuroscience community.

From the onset of my scientific career, I have worked to develop hardware and software to assist the neuroscience community. Below is a list of some of these contributions and ongoing projects:

a.     Simultaneous dopamine and single-unit/local-field measurement. Awarded a 2014 NSF BRAIN EAGER grant to develop technologies for the simultaneous recording of the activities of ensembles of neurons and real-time measurement of dopamine release. Since receiving support, we have produced working versions of this device and successfully tested the device in anesthetized and awake and behaving rats (Parent et al., 2017). The next stage of development will be to improve the hardware and software to improve robustness and ease of use.
Contribution: Experiment design, software development, data analysis and discussion, wrote manuscript.

b.     Improving voltammetric measurement of dopamine concentration by reducing the effects of biofouling (Seaton et al., 2020) and improving safety and FDA compliance (Siegenthaler et al., 2020). Contribution: Experimental and facilities support, review of manuscript.

c.      Ultrasound measurement of electrical brain activity. I am a collaborator on Brain Initiative R24 (Lead PI: Russel Witte, U of A) to develop a non-invasive ultrasound system for the measurement of electrical activity in the brain. The system capitalizes on the acoustoelectric effect, and my role is to validate the system’s effectiveness by comparing in vivo measurements obtained from the ultrasound system with measurements obtained from traditional electrophysiology.
Contribution: Experiment design, software development, data analysis and discussion
